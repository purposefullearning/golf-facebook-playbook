Context

The Playing Field Is More Level Than You Think

You don't need a press pass to build a golf audience

The PGA TOUR, Golf Channel, and Golf Digest all rely on exclusive media access — tournament credentials, broadcast rights, and player interviews — that independent creators simply cannot replicate. But the data tells a different story about what actually drives audience growth. Rick Shiels (1.5M followers) outgrew both Golf Channel and Golf Digest by focusing on what golfers actually want: gear knowledge, swing fixes, relatable humor, and honest opinions. The content angles below require nothing more than a smartphone, a golf club, and a willingness to show up consistently.

Actionable Strategy

8 Content Angles That Work Without Media Access

Every one of these can be executed with a smartphone and access to any golf course or driving range

ANGLE 01

Equipment Reviews & Gear Tests

Every golfer wants to know if a new club, ball, or training aid is worth buying. Buy or borrow gear, test it honestly on camera, and give a real verdict. Compare budget options to premium ones. The more honest and specific, the better.

Rick Shiels: "Golf Shaft That Goes 50 Yards Longer" — 13.9K views
ANGLE 02

The "What Would You Do?" Scenario Post

Golf has endless rules situations, course management dilemmas, and etiquette debates. Show a tricky lie, an unusual ruling, or a tough course management decision and ask the audience. This format turns passive viewers into active commenters instantly.

Rick Shiels: "What would you do?" — 152,300 views, 343 reactions
ANGLE 03

Skill Challenges & Debates

"Who hits it further?" "Who has the best short game?" These are inherently shareable because people tag friends to debate. Challenge a friend, challenge yourself, or challenge the audience to try it. The competition format drives comments and shares.

Good Good: "Who has the best stinger?" — 182,700 views, 986 reactions
ANGLE 04

The Improvement Journey

Golfers are obsessed with improvement. Document your journey from high handicap to low, track your handicap publicly, and share honest struggles and breakthroughs. Long-term audience loyalty comes from people who feel invested in your progress.

Relatable content builds the deepest audience loyalty of any format
ANGLE 05

Pro Swing Analysis

Analyze any pro's swing using publicly available broadcast footage or YouTube clips. No credentials needed — just knowledge and a good eye. Overlay slow-motion analysis, explain one key takeaway per video, and connect it to what amateurs can learn.

Me & My Golf: "Adam Scott Swing Thought" — 25,300 views, 81 reactions
ANGLE 06

Humor, Bloopers & Relatable Fails

Golf is inherently funny — bad shots, course disasters, etiquette fails. Film your own mishits, funny moments on the course, and honest reactions to poor play. Relatable content that every golfer has experienced is the most shareable format in the space.

Good Good: "Matt Scharff gets put on BLAST" — 124,900 views, 2,200 reactions
ANGLE 07

Course Reviews & Hidden Gems

Golfers are always looking for new courses to play. A genuine review from a fellow golfer feels more trustworthy than a paid media outlet. Focus on value, conditions, and character. Play a course, film key holes, and give an honest verdict.

No media access needed — just book a tee time and bring a camera
ANGLE 08

Curiosity & Trivia Hooks

"Did you know...?" content gets massive reach because people share it to show off their golf knowledge. Dig into unusual equipment facts, historical moments, rule oddities, or pro player quirks. The hook is the headline — make it impossible not to click.

Rick Shiels: "How Bryson DeChambeau marks his golf ball" — 14,800 views
Execution

The Independent Creator Growth Playbook

Principles drawn from studying Rick Shiels, Good Good, and Me and My Golf

📅

Post Every Day, No Exceptions

All three independent creators post daily regardless of the tournament calendar. Me and My Golf posts every 2–4 hours. Rick Shiels posts multiple times per day. The algorithm rewards consistency over quality spikes. Show up every day and the algorithm will show up for you.

🎯

Name the Problem in the Title

Me and My Golf's most-searched content uses hyper-specific fault-based titles: "Stop swinging over the top," "How to create lag," "Fix your early extension." Golfers search for their specific problem. If your title names it exactly, you win the click every time.

💬

Engineer Comments, Not Just Views

Rick Shiels' highest-engagement posts are questions, not statements. "What would you do in this situation?" generates 10x more comments than a straight tip video. Ask for opinions, spark debates, and invite disagreement. Comments signal to the algorithm that content is worth distributing.

🎬

Keep It Short and Vertical

Every top-performing video across all three independent pages is under 2 minutes. Most are under 60 seconds. Facebook Reels (vertical, short-form) now receive the highest organic reach of any format on the platform. Film vertically, edit tightly, and get to the point in the first 3 seconds.

🔁

Build Series, Not One-Off Posts

Me and My Golf's "named drill series" (band drills, impact fixes, early extension fixes) creates return viewers who come back for the next installment. A series gives your audience a reason to follow you rather than just watch a single video. Name your recurring formats and promote them consistently.

🤝

Collaborate to Grow Faster

Good Good's fastest growth came from creator collaborations and challenge matches. Rick Shiels' most-shared content involves other personalities. Collaborating with other golf creators — even small ones — exposes you to their audience and signals social proof to the algorithm. Reach out to creators at your level and propose a challenge.

🛒

Build a Funnel, Not Just a Following

Me and My Golf uses Facebook as a top-of-funnel machine that drives golfers into paid coaching plans. Good Good uses content to sell merchandise. Rick Shiels monetizes through YouTube AdSense and brand deals. A following without a funnel is just a vanity metric — decide early what you want your audience to do next.

📊

Double Down on What Works

When Rick Shiels' "What would you do?" post hit 152K views, the lesson was clear: scenario content outperforms instruction content for reach. When Good Good's roast clips hit 2.2K reactions, they made more roast clips. Check your insights weekly and ruthlessly replicate your top performers rather than chasing novelty.
